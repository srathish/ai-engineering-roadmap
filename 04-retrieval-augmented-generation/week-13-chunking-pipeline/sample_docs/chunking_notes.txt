Notes on Chunking Strategies

Chunking is the step where a document is cut into smaller pieces before
embedding. It sounds trivial but it has an outsized effect on retrieval
quality. Chunks that are too large dilute the embedding with unrelated
content. Chunks that are too small lose the surrounding context that gives a
sentence its meaning.

The simplest strategy is fixed-size chunking. You slide a window of a fixed
number of characters or tokens across the text and emit each window as a
chunk. To stop a sentence from being cut in half at a boundary, you let
consecutive windows overlap by a small amount. Fifty to a hundred characters
of overlap is a common starting point.

A more careful strategy is sentence or paragraph aware chunking. Here you
respect natural boundaries: you never split in the middle of a sentence, and
you try to keep paragraphs together. You pack whole sentences into a chunk
until you reach a size budget, then start a new chunk. Carrying one sentence
of overlap between chunks keeps a thread of continuity.

There is no single best chunk size. It depends on the documents and the
questions. Short factual snippets favor small chunks. Narrative or technical
explanations favor larger ones. The honest answer is that you measure
retrieval quality on real questions and tune from there.
